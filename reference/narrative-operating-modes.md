# Narrative Operating Modes

Use this guide whenever a skill turns planning artifacts into prose or evaluates prose quality. The goal is to keep the proposal from sounding like a sequence of validated facts stitched together by process language.

## Purpose

Every proposal needs a prose strategy before drafting begins. The strategy is set by the proposal type, page target, submission mechanism, and evaluator framing in `working/proposal-type.md`.

The proposal should read like one argument scaled to the available space:

- A short white paper is a compressed business case.
- A CSO or OTA brief is a fast screen for fit, value, risk, and next action.
- A FAR technical volume is a scorable response with enough narrative flow that the evaluator can remember why the approach matters.
- An SBIR or BAA response is a technical merit case with commercialization or transition logic.

Do not use the same prose rhythm for all four.

## Mode Selection

Choose one mode before storyboarding, drafting, or editing.

| Mode | Typical trigger | Reader need | Prose posture |
|---|---|---|---|
| `compressed-brief` | 1-5 pages, web-form fields, short white paper, directed brief | Grasp the idea in one skim and decide whether to continue | Verdict first, concrete, few abstractions, no setup paragraphs |
| `solution-brief` | 5-10 pages, CSO brief, OTA white paper, invited concept paper | Decide whether the concept is credible enough for a meeting, prototype, or Phase 2 | Problem -> approach -> proof -> next action, with selective technical depth |
| `scored-volume` | FAR RFP, IDIQ task order, CSO full, OTA full | Find required answers fast and assign strengths | Section M aware, traceable, explicit, but still connected by the spine |
| `technical-merit` | SBIR, BAA, research proposal | Judge feasibility, novelty, work plan, and transition | Technical claim first, mechanism second, evidence and risk controls close behind |
| `market-research` | RFI, sources sought | Determine relevance and shape acquisition thinking | Direct answers, no win-theme theatrics, no sales arc |
| `pricing-artifact` | ROM, price narrative, budget narrative | Understand range, assumptions, and cost drivers | Sparse, numeric, assumptions visible, no persuasion beyond credibility |

When in doubt, select the shorter, plainer mode. Overexplaining is the default failure mode.

## Mode Rules

### compressed-brief

- Start with the answer, not background.
- Use the customer's problem in the first paragraph.
- Keep paragraphs to 2-4 sentences.
- Prefer one strong graphic over several supporting graphics.
- Use bullets only when they compress information better than prose.
- Avoid concept teaching, methodology background, and company history.
- Every section must change what the reader believes after the executive summary; otherwise cut it.

### solution-brief

- The opening page must answer: what is offered, why it matters now, why it is credible, and what should happen next.
- Explain the solution as a customer outcome before naming architecture components.
- Use proof points where they reduce adoption risk, not as a resume dump.
- Make the pilot, prototype, or next step concrete.
- Technical detail earns space only when it proves feasibility, integration realism, or transition value.

### scored-volume

- Keep the solicitation's structure findable, but do not let headings become the story.
- Open each scoring section with the evaluator's concern and the proposed answer.
- Use the narrative spine to connect sections so the volume reads as one case, not a compliance binder.
- Put proof points before the evaluator has to ask for them.
- Close long sections with a short evaluator takeaway when the section exceeds about two pages.

### technical-merit

- State the technical hypothesis or core mechanism early.
- Distinguish known capability, proposed development, and research risk.
- Show why the work plan will prove or de-risk the claim.
- Avoid product brochure language; technical reviewers distrust it.
- Tie novelty to transition or operational value, not novelty for its own sake.

### market-research

- Answer each question directly and in order.
- Do not force a win theme or competitor contrast.
- Use factual capability statements and relevant examples.
- Signal constraints honestly; market research rewards useful truth.

### pricing-artifact

- Lead with the range, basis of estimate, and assumptions.
- Do not bury exclusions or validity limits.
- Use narrative only to explain cost drivers and risk controls.
- Avoid value claims that belong in the technical or executive narrative.

## The Reader-Movement Test

For every planned or drafted section, identify the movement it creates:

1. What does the reader believe before this section?
2. What should the reader believe after it?
3. What proof or explanation causes that shift?
4. What should the reader do with that belief when scoring or deciding?

If the section does not move the reader, it is either redundant, misplaced, or too abstract.

## Transition Rules

Robotic proposals usually fail in transitions. They move from artifact to artifact instead of idea to idea.

Use transitions that do one of four jobs:

- **Consequence:** "That constraint changes the architecture..."
- **Selection:** "For that reason, we propose..."
- **Proof:** "This is not a paper design..."
- **Decision:** "The practical next step is..."

Avoid transitions that merely announce structure:

- "This section discusses..."
- "The following paragraphs describe..."
- "In addition, it is important to note..."
- "As stated above..."

## Compression Rules

When page pressure exists, cut in this order:

1. Generic setup sentences.
2. Repeated company positioning.
3. Abstract concept explanation.
4. Process language not tied to evaluator risk.
5. Secondary proof points.
6. Technical detail not needed for credibility or scoring.

Preserve in this order:

1. Compliance-required content.
2. Customer-stated problem language.
3. The narrative spine's through-line.
4. Discriminator proof points.
5. Explicit commitments and assumptions.
6. Graphics and captions that carry the argument.

## Red Flags

- The first paragraph could fit a competitor with only the company name changed.
- The draft explains the process before the offer.
- The reader reaches page two before knowing what is being proposed.
- Every section has the same opening rhythm.
- The proposal says "we will leverage" more often than it says what will be delivered.
- The editor can cut a paragraph without changing the argument.
- A white paper teaches the entire market before making the recommendation.
